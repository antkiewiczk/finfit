# Investing 101: A Beginner's Guide to Building Wealth

Investing is one of the most powerful tools for building long-term wealth. While it may seem intimidating at first, understanding the basics can help you make informed decisions and grow your money over time.

## Why Invest?

### The Power of Compound Interest
Compound interest is often called the "eighth wonder of the world." When you invest, your money earns returns, and those returns then earn their own returns. Over decades, this creates exponential growth.

**Example**: If you invest $10,000 with an average 7% annual return:
- After 10 years: $19,672
- After 20 years: $38,697
- After 30 years: $76,123

### Beating Inflation
Inflation erodes the purchasing power of your money. If your money sits in a savings account earning 1% while inflation is 3%, you're effectively losing 2% per year. Investing helps your money grow faster than inflation.

## Investment Vehicles

### 1. Stocks (Equities)
Stocks represent ownership in a company. When you buy a stock, you own a small piece of that business.

**Pros**:
- High growth potential
- Dividend income possible
- Liquidity (easy to buy/sell)

**Cons**:
- Higher volatility
- Risk of loss
- Requires research

### 2. Bonds (Fixed Income)
Bonds are loans you make to companies or governments. They pay regular interest and return your principal at maturity.

**Pros**:
- Regular income
- Lower risk than stocks
- Predictable returns

**Cons**:
- Lower growth potential
- Interest rate risk
- Inflation risk

### 3. Mutual Funds
Mutual funds pool money from many investors to buy a diversified portfolio of stocks, bonds, or other securities.

**Pros**:
- Instant diversification
- Professional management
- Accessible for small investors

**Cons**:
- Management fees
- Less control over holdings
- Minimum investments may apply

### 4. ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds)
ETFs are similar to mutual funds but trade like stocks on exchanges.

**Pros**:
- Lower fees than mutual funds
- Intraday trading
- Tax efficiency

**Cons**:
- Trading commissions (sometimes)
- Bid-ask spreads
- May track indexes passively

## Getting Started

### Step 1: Define Your Goals
- **Short-term** (1-3 years): Emergency fund, vacation, down payment
- **Medium-term** (3-10 years): Home purchase, education, business startup
- **Long-term** (10+ years): Retirement, legacy building

### Step 2: Assess Your Risk Tolerance
Ask yourself:
- How would I react if my portfolio dropped 20%?
- When will I need this money?
- What's my investment knowledge level?

### Step 3: Choose Your Account Type
- **Taxable brokerage**: For general investing
- **IRA**: For retirement (tax advantages)
- **401(k)**: Employer-sponsored retirement plan
- **529 Plan**: For education savings

### Step 4: Start Small
You don't need thousands to begin. Many platforms allow you to start with:
- Fractional shares
- Low-cost index funds
- Automatic monthly investments

## Investment Strategies

### Dollar-Cost Averaging
Investing a fixed amount regularly, regardless of market conditions. This reduces the impact of volatility and removes emotion from investing.

**Example**: Investing $500 every month in an S&P 500 index fund.

### Asset Allocation
Dividing your portfolio among different asset classes based on your goals and risk tolerance.

**Conservative**: 60% bonds, 40% stocks
**Moderate**: 50% stocks, 40% bonds, 10% cash
**Aggressive**: 80% stocks, 15% bonds, 5% cash

### Diversification
"Don't put all your eggs in one basket." Spread your investments across:
- Different asset classes
- Various industries
- Multiple geographic regions

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Trying to Time the Market
Even professional investors struggle with market timing. Focus on time IN the market, not timing the market.

### 2. Letting Emotions Drive Decisions
Fear and greed are investors' worst enemies. Create a plan and stick to it.

### 3. Chasing Past Performance
Just because an investment did well last year doesn't mean it will this year.

### 4. Ignoring Fees
High fees can significantly erode returns over time. Look for low-cost options.

### 5. Not Rebalancing
Regularly rebalance your portfolio to maintain your target asset allocation.

## Sample Beginner Portfolio

### Simple Three-Fund Portfolio
1. **Total US Stock Market ETF**: 50%
2. **Total International Stock ETF**: 30%
3. **Total Bond Market ETF**: 20%

This provides global diversification with minimal complexity.

### Robo-Advisor Option
For hands-off investors, robo-advisors like:
- Betterment
- Wealthfront
- Acorns

These automatically manage your portfolio for a small fee.

## Monitoring Your Investments

### What to Track
- Overall portfolio performance
- Individual investment performance
- Asset allocation
- Fees and expenses

### When to Review
- **Monthly**: Check progress toward goals
- **Quarterly**: Review asset allocation
- **Annually**: Rebalance if needed

### What NOT to Do
- Check prices daily (causes anxiety)
- Make frequent changes
- React to short-term news
